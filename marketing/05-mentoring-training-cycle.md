# Mentoring & Training Cycle

## 44. Coaching Checklist
A tool that tracks whether a concept has been heard, obeyed, shared, and taught provides mentors with a structured way to measure progress. By using this systematic approach, mentors can identify areas needing further development and adjust training accordingly. The checklist encourages accountability for each step of discipleship, ensuring that learning does not stop at knowledge alone. It also promotes transparency, as both mentor and mentee can review progress together. The tool fosters continuous improvement by highlighting successes and gaps in the learning process. Ultimately, this checklist supports intentional growth for both mentors and mentees within the church community.

## 45. Coaching Checklist – Heard
This column asks, "Did the mentee hear about this tool or concept?" It ensures foundational awareness before moving to practice, preventing misconceptions. By confirming that the mentee is informed, mentors can tailor explanations and resources more effectively. The column also serves as a baseline for measuring engagement over time. It encourages mentees to seek clarification if anything is unclear, fostering open communication. By tracking "heard," mentors can quickly identify and address gaps in knowledge early in the training process. Ultimately, this step establishes a solid foundation for subsequent learning stages.

## 46. Coaching Checklist – Obeyed
The mentee reflects whether they have practiced the concept, underscoring that faith must be lived, not merely understood. By evaluating personal application, mentors assess the depth of commitment and identify obstacles to action. This reflection promotes accountability, as mentees openly share successes or struggles with their mentor. It also provides an opportunity to adjust strategies and offer targeted support. By focusing on obedience, the process moves beyond theoretical knowledge into tangible transformation. Ultimately, this column reinforces that discipleship requires intentional practice and consistent effort.

## 47. Coaching Checklist – Shared
The mentee considers whether they have taught others what they learned, demonstrating mastery through teaching. By sharing, the mentee reinforces their own understanding and extends God's message to a broader audience. This step encourages a culture of generosity, where knowledge is passed on rather than hoarded. It also provides a practical way to measure the impact of training within the community. Mentors can use this data to identify those ready for higher levels of responsibility. Ultimately, sharing embodies the Great Commission by turning learning into action.

## 48. Coaching Checklist – Trained
The mentee has trained someone else on the concept, completing the cycle of learning, practicing, sharing, and teaching. By stepping into a trainer role, the mentee demonstrates competence and confidence in their skills. This experience not only consolidates their own knowledge but also expands the training network within the church. It encourages a self‑sustaining model where each graduate becomes an instructor for future learners. Mentors can celebrate these milestones as evidence of growth and multiplication. Ultimately, training others solidifies the mentee's position as a leader in discipleship.

## 49. Mentee Development Level – Unaware
The mentee has not yet heard of the tool or concept, and awareness is the first step toward transformation. By recognizing this status, mentors can prioritize introductory education to build foundational knowledge. This stage involves presenting the core principles and benefits in a clear, accessible manner. It also invites questions that clarify misunderstandings before moving deeper into practice. By establishing a baseline of awareness, mentors set the stage for meaningful engagement. Ultimately, being unaware is an opportunity to spark curiosity and initiate a discipleship journey.

## 50. Mentee Development Level – Unskilled
The mentee knows the tool but lacks confidence or competence, requiring further coaching to build skill. Mentors should provide targeted exercises that allow hands‑on practice and constructive feedback. By creating a supportive learning environment, mentees can experiment without fear of failure. Regular check‑ins help track progress and address specific challenges promptly. This phase emphasizes gradual mastery, reinforcing that competence grows through repetition and guidance. Ultimately, developing skill transforms knowledge into reliable action.

## 51. Mentee Development Level – Competent
The mentee can perform the basics of the tool and is ready to practice independently, signaling that foundational learning has been achieved. At this stage, mentors should focus on refining technique and encouraging creative application in varied contexts. By challenging the mentee with realistic scenarios, they can deepen understanding and confidence. Mentors also help set personal goals that extend beyond routine tasks. This level promotes self‑reliance while maintaining accountability for continued growth. Ultimately, competence allows the mentee to contribute effectively to their community.

## 52. Mentee Development Level – Skilled
The mentee is confident and can train others, marking the end of formal mentoring. This level demonstrates mastery that empowers the individual to lead and influence peers positively. Mentors should now focus on leadership development, guiding the mentee in coaching techniques and strategic vision. By entrusting them with training responsibilities, they cultivate a culture of multiplication within the church. The mentee also becomes a role model for new learners, embodying the principles they teach. Ultimately, skilled status signifies readiness to sustain and grow discipleship beyond the initial mentor relationship.

## 53. Mentor Role – Model
The mentor demonstrates the tool, giving a concrete example that learners can emulate. By modeling authentic practice, mentors provide clarity and reduce confusion about implementation. This visible example also builds trust, as mentees see the mentor living out the principles in real life. It encourages learners to internalize techniques through observation and imitation. The mentor's example serves as a living teaching tool that bridges theory and practice. Ultimately, modeling ensures learners have a tangible reference point for their own growth.

## 54. Mentor Role – Assist
While the learner practices, the mentor provides guidance and support, ensuring foundational competence. By offering constructive feedback, mentors help learners correct mistakes before they become habits. This hands‑on assistance fosters confidence and a sense of safety in the learning environment. Mentors also encourage reflective thinking, prompting learners to analyze what works and why. The support phase balances structure with autonomy, enabling the learner to take ownership gradually. Ultimately, assistance builds a solid skill base that prepares for independent application.

## 55. Mentor Role – Watch
The mentor observes the learner's progress, offering feedback until competence is achieved. This observation phase allows mentors to gauge subtle nuances that may escape the learner's awareness. By noting strengths and areas for improvement, mentors tailor subsequent coaching to specific needs. The mentor also models patience, giving the learner time to internalize lessons fully. Continuous observation ensures that learning remains relevant and effective over time. Ultimately, this watchful stance supports sustained growth and mastery.

## 56. Mentor Role – Leave
The mentor graduates the learner, allowing independence while remaining available for occasional guidance. By granting autonomy, mentors acknowledge the mentee's readiness to apply skills independently. This transition encourages self‑reliance and confidence in decision making. The mentor still offers resources for advanced challenges, ensuring continued development. Leaving the formal relationship marks a milestone of trust and responsibility. Ultimately, this step empowers mentees to become leaders who can train others in turn.

## 57. Mentor Behaviors – Direct and Inform
Clear instruction is essential; the mentor explicitly explains expectations, processes, and objectives. By providing detailed guidance, mentors reduce ambiguity that can hinder progress. The direct approach also sets a tone of transparency and accountability. It ensures learners understand the "why" behind each step, fostering deeper engagement. Mentors may also anticipate common questions to pre‑empt confusion. Ultimately, direct and informative behavior lays a solid foundation for effective learning.

## 58. Mentor Behaviors – Direct and Support
The mentor offers guidance while encouraging the learner's independent decision‑making, balancing structure with autonomy. By giving constructive feedback and resources, mentors empower learners to apply concepts creatively. This dual approach nurtures confidence while maintaining accountability. The mentor also models problem‑solving skills that learners can emulate. Encouragement reinforces motivation, helping learners persist through challenges. Ultimately, this supportive style cultivates self‑sufficiency and resilience.

## 59. Mentor Behaviors – Support and Encourage
Positive reinforcement fuels confidence, especially when learners struggle. By celebrating milestones and progress, mentors keep motivation high. Encouragement also builds a safe environment where mistakes are viewed as learning opportunities. The mentor may share personal anecdotes to illustrate resilience and growth. This supportive stance helps learners internalize the value of perseverance. Ultimately, encouragement strengthens commitment to long‑term discipleship.

## 60. Mentor Behaviors – Receive Updates
The mentor stays informed about the learner's progress, allowing timely feedback and support. Regular updates help mentors track growth trajectories and adjust instruction accordingly. By staying connected, mentors can anticipate challenges before they become obstacles. These updates also reinforce accountability and transparency between mentor and mentee. The process encourages open communication, fostering trust in the relationship. Ultimately, receiving updates ensures that mentoring remains responsive and effective.

## 61. Planning Responsibilities – Mentor decides
The mentor sets the plan, using experience to tailor tasks to the learner's needs. By taking ownership of planning, mentors can prioritize critical milestones and resources. This approach ensures that learning objectives align with overall discipleship goals. The mentor also establishes clear timelines and measurable outcomes. By controlling the plan, mentors maintain flexibility to adapt strategies as progress unfolds. Ultimately, mentor‑driven planning provides structure and direction for effective development.

## 62. Planning Responsibilities – Discuss & Mentor decides
The mentor and mentee collaborate, but the mentor retains final authority. This collaborative process respects the learner's input while ensuring decisions remain aligned with biblical principles. By jointly reviewing goals, mentors can incorporate the learner's insights into a cohesive strategy. The mentor maintains responsibility for approving the final plan, guaranteeing consistency and coherence. This approach balances partnership with professional judgment. Ultimately, it empowers the learner while preserving a clear direction for growth.

## 63. Planning Responsibilities – Discuss & Mentee decides
The mentee leads the planning process, with the mentor providing guidance. By taking initiative, the learner demonstrates ownership and responsibility for their growth. The mentor offers feedback to refine objectives and clarify expectations. This collaborative dynamic encourages the mentee to think critically about priorities and resources. The mentor's guidance ensures that plans remain realistic and spiritually sound. Ultimately, mentee‑led planning fosters self‑confidence and proactive discipleship.

## 64. Planning Responsibilities – Mentee decides
The mentee independently creates the plan, with the mentor offering minimal oversight. This stage reflects full maturity and self‑direction in discipleship. By drafting their own objectives, the learner demonstrates deep commitment and accountability. The mentor's role is to review for feasibility without imposing direction, allowing authentic decision‑making. This autonomy encourages the mentee to manage resources and timelines effectively. Ultimately, independent planning showcases readiness for sustained leadership.

## 75. The Training Cycle – Model
Modeling demonstrates a new skill or concept, giving the learner a concrete example that can be emulated. By observing an instructor perform the task, learners gain clarity on expected outcomes and techniques. The model also establishes a benchmark for quality and accuracy in the learner's future attempts. This visual demonstration bridges theory with practice, reducing confusion that might arise from verbal instruction alone. By providing a live example, mentors can address questions immediately and tailor explanations to observed challenges. Ultimately, modeling ensures that learners have a clear reference point for their own growth.

## 76. The Training Cycle – Assist
During assisting, the mentor hands‑holds while the learner practices, ensuring foundational competence. By offering real‑time feedback, mentors help learners correct errors before they become habits. This supportive role also builds confidence, as the learner feels guided rather than judged. The mentor may pause to explain nuances and provide resources for deeper understanding. By actively participating, the learner benefits from a safe environment that encourages experimentation. Ultimately, assisting transforms theoretical knowledge into practical skill through guided practice.

## 77. The Training Cycle – Watch
Watching allows the mentor to observe the learner's progress, intervening only when necessary, fostering independence. By monitoring technique and application, mentors can identify subtle issues early on. This observational approach also encourages the learner to self‑evaluate and adjust their practice accordingly. The mentor offers constructive feedback that focuses on improvement rather than criticism. By stepping back, the learner gains autonomy and responsibility for their own development. Ultimately, watching balances guidance with freedom, promoting sustainable growth.

## 78. The Training Cycle – Leave
Leaving signifies that the learner can perform the task independently, while mentors remain available for occasional guidance. This transition signals that the learner has reached a level of competence where self‑directed practice is appropriate. Mentors may set clear expectations for continued progress, such as regular check‑ins or milestone reviews, to maintain accountability. The learner's increased responsibility fosters ownership and encourages proactive problem‑solving. Meanwhile, mentors continue to serve as resourceful advisors—offering advanced techniques or deeper insights when requested. Ultimately, the leave step marks a milestone of trust and maturity, positioning learners to become future mentors themselves.

## 89. The Training Cycle – Model
The "Model" step is the first, most visible phase of the training cycle. During this stage, the mentor demonstrates a new skill or concept in real time, providing an exact blueprint for learners to follow. By observing the mentor's actions, participants can see how theory translates into practice, reducing uncertainty and building confidence. The model also establishes a performance standard; learners know what quality looks like and can benchmark their own attempts against it. Importantly, modeling allows mentors to correct subtle mistakes immediately—such as posture or tone—that could otherwise become ingrained habits. Finally, the visual demonstration creates a shared reference point that learners can revisit throughout their practice, ensuring consistency and reinforcing learning.

## 90. The Training Cycle – Assist
In the "Assist" phase, mentors shift from demonstration to active guidance while learners apply what they've seen. This hands‑on support includes real‑time feedback, gentle corrections, and encouragement tailored to each learner's pace. By assisting, mentors help learners internalize correct techniques before they form habits, preventing the formation of errors that could hinder future performance. The assist stage also fosters a sense of partnership; learners feel supported rather than judged, which boosts motivation and reduces anxiety. Moreover, the mentor can identify specific learning gaps during assistance—such as a recurring mispronunciation or incomplete concept—and address them immediately. Ultimately, this collaborative practice turns abstract instruction into tangible competence, paving the way for independent mastery.

## 91. The Training Cycle – Watch
The "Watch" step allows the mentor to observe learners as they practice independently, intervening only when necessary. By stepping back, mentors provide learners with the autonomy to experiment and self‑correct, which is essential for building confidence. Observation also enables mentors to spot subtle progress or persistent challenges that may not be obvious during direct instruction. When the mentor does intervene, it is typically brief and targeted—focusing on fine‑tuning rather than re‑teaching fundamentals. This approach respects the learner's growing independence while maintaining a safety net of expert guidance. Over time, the watch phase helps solidify skills into automaticity and prepares learners for real‑world application.

## 92. The Training Cycle – Leave
In the "Leave" phase, mentors formally release learners to apply their skills independently while remaining available for occasional support. This transition signals that the learner has reached a level of competence where self‑directed practice is appropriate. Mentors may set clear expectations for continued progress, such as regular check‑ins or milestone reviews, to maintain accountability. The learner's increased responsibility fosters ownership and encourages proactive problem‑solving. Meanwhile, mentors continue to serve as resourceful advisors—offering advanced techniques or deeper insights when requested. Ultimately, the leave step marks a milestone of trust and maturity, positioning learners to become future mentors themselves.
